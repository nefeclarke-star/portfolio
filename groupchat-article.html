The Group Chat Is Doing More Than Your Press Release

By Nefe Clarke · Digital Engagement

The most effective climate communication network in my city is not a media house. It is a WhatsApp group.

When the water starts rising in parts of Lagos, the first alerts don't come from radio. They come from neighbors. Someone posts a video of the street. Someone else names which roads to avoid. Within an hour, hundreds of people have rerouted their day. No budget, no comms strategy, no approval chain. Just people who trust each other, moving information fast.

Meanwhile, plenty of organizations doing serious climate work are still communicating like it's 2009. Long press releases. PDFs nobody opens. A social media page that posts event flyers and goes quiet for three weeks.

Young Africans are the most connected generation this continent has ever produced, and they have built their own information systems: group chats, voice notes, memes, short video. If you want to engage them, you go where the conversation already lives. You make content people actually want to forward, because on this continent, forwarding is publishing.

This is not about abandoning formal communication. Donors read reports, and reports matter. It is about knowing that a farmer will trust a voice note from someone who sounds like him before he trusts a glossy infographic in English.

The organizations that will shape the next decade of climate action are the ones humble enough to learn from the group chat. Speed, trust, and language people recognize. That is the whole formula, and young people already have it.
